Introduction

Yanglix is the sacred language gifted to the Kypwatchidwax by the legendary King Pwatchidwax. According to the first book of the Yqmaqmiax, the King spoke the First Word in the darkness before the world formed.

Every word in Yanglix is therefore treated as spiritually significant. To speak is to recreate the King's creative act, and many ceremonial traditions revolve around the careful recitation of sacred text.

Historically, Yanglix was reserved only for ritual speech, administration, and legal inscriptions. A simplified form later developed for daily use, but the classical register is still preserved in scripture and performance.

The Phonological Inventory and Orthography

Yanglix uses a distinctive orthography based on a modified Latin script. Although written with the same letters familiar to English users, several letters represent different or shifted sounds. Consonants dominate the sound system due to the mythic tradition that the First Word itself was spoken without vowels.

Consonant Letters and Their Core Values

Yanglix traditionally recognizes the following consonants:

  • Aa - used mainly for /a/ or schwa in liturgical pronunciation
  • Bb - /b/
  • Cc - /k/ or /tʃ/ when doubled (cc → /tʃ/)
  • Dd - /d/
  • Gg - /g/
  • Ii - functions historically as both vowel /i/ and consonant /j/
  • Jj - /dʒ/
  • Kk - /k/
  • Mm - /m/
  • Nn - /n/
  • Oo - reduced /o/ or /u/ depending on environment
  • Pp - /p/
  • Qq - a deeper /q/ sound used in sacred reading
  • Ss - pronounced /ts/
  • Tt - /t/ → becomes /d/ after /n/ and unstressed
  • Ww - /w/
  • Xx - /ks/ or /kʃ/ in careful speech
  • Yy - /j/, often silent after tense consonants
  • Zz - /dz/

Vowels and Sonorants

While Yanglix is not fully vowelless, vowels serve a supportive role. In rapid ceremonial recitation, many vowels reduce or disappear entirely, giving the language a sharp, rhythmic cadence. Orthographically, A, I, and O are the primary vowel carriers.

Phonological Rules and Allophony

  • y becomes silent when following a tense stop
  • cc always represents /tʃ/, never /kk/
  • t becomes /d/ after n in unstressed position
  • Clusters with s retain /ts/, even word-finally

Phonotactics and Suprasegmentals

Syllable Structure

Yanglix allows dense consonant clusters with the common template:

(C)(C)V(C)(C)

Many words may be written without an overt vowel, but a neutral schwa-like vowel is often inserted for ease of pronunciation in slower or formal speech.

Stress

Stress is generally penultimate (on the second-to-last syllable). However, sacred recitation permits stylized rhythmic stress patterns that override normal rules.

Rhythm and Tone

Yanglix is not tonal, but pitch contour matters in ritual. A rising contour symbolizes invocation, while a falling contour symbolizes closure or finality.

Introductory Syntax

Yanglix is highly flexible in its word order. The meaning of a sentence is carried primarily through context and particles rather than strict structural positioning. While Classical Yanglix frequently prefers SOV in ceremonial text, everyday speech freely shifts to SVO, OSV, or other permutations.

Many grammarians recommend consistency of ordering within a single discourse to avoid ambiguity, but technically there are no rules that forbid free reordering.

Negation

The suffix -bya marks negation:

xakky-bya → "not happy"

Demonstrative Examples

"Ya xakkybya."
ya xakky-bya
1SG happy-NEG
“I am sad.”
"Xakkybya ya."
xakky-bya ya
happy-NEG 1SG
“I am sad.”

Pronouns

Yanglix has an extremely simplified pronominal system. The language only has two pronouns, and one of them is optional in most contexts.

  • ya - universal personal pronoun, referring to any person or thing
  • koog - optional second-person pronoun meaning “you” when the speaker wishes to specify

ya is the default pronoun and can mean:

  • I
  • you
  • he / she / they
  • it
  • we (context usually clarifies)

Because ya is universal, Yanglix sentences rely on context, grammar, and social knowledge to interpret who is being referenced.

Cultural Note

In the mythology of Yanglix, King Pwatchidwax granted language to mankind with a single pronoun. At the time of creation, everyone was understood in relation to everyone else, so ambiguity was not considered a problem. The language preserves this divine simplicity by avoiding pronoun inflation.

koog - Specific “You”

koog is used only when the speaker wants to emphasize them. It specifies that the subject is the listener directly.

However, it is used sparingly. In most speech, ya still means “you” unless clarification is required.

Examples

"Ya xamgy."
ya xamgy
1/2/3.PRS speak
“I am speaking.” / “You are speaking.” / “He is speaking.” (Context determines.)
"Koog xamgy."
koog xamgy
2SG speak
“You are speaking.” (Explicit.)
"Ya taakya ya."
ya taak-ya ya
1SG see-PST 1SG
“I saw him/her/it/them.” / “I saw myself.”

Ambiguity as Feature

Because Yanglix usually does not specify grammatical person, the language encourages:

  • flexibility in interpretation
  • poetic and metaphorical phrasing
  • focus on action rather than grammatical roles

Whether a sentence means “I am speaking” or “You are speaking” is something expected to be understood from situational or conversational context but not grammar.
